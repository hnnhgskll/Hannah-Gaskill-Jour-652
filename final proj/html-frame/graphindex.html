Online Dating: A Brief History

With the advent of newspapers came the birth of the personal ad. According to the Huffington Post, after its debut in 1695 the personal ad was a way for British men to find mates, but quickly after the turn of the century it evolved into something more subversive–a way for homosexual men to find partners through coded language.

Into the 1800s, aristocrats began using newspaper personal ads with salacious undertones to find partners, and the general public began to catch on, resulting in the normalization of the ads in magazines and periodicals. Fast-forwarding to the 1900’s, personal ads became popular in the United States, acting as a potential panacea for those cold and lonely pioneer nights in the western frontier, and as some potential pen-pal companionship as lonely soldiers were shipped overseas during WWI.

It wasn’t until 1965, the Huffington Post reports, that a group of Harvard undergrads created the first computerized dating service: Operation Match. For a small fee, singles could fill out a questionnaire that would pair them with a list of other singles–not too far off from the algorithms used by modern-day apps.

The ‘90s is the decade that brought us online personals and chatting platforms, such as AOL and Craigslist, which sufficed until Match.com–the first modern-day dating service, and still one of the most popular–was created in 1995, and with the new millennium came an influx of dating services that millions of people swipe left and right on today.

"We use math to find you dates."


When signing up for OkCupid–or any dating app, for that matter–one of the first things that users are required to do is assign themselves a gender. On most of the popular dating apps (Tinder, Bumble, etc.), users abide by the gender binary, choosing solely between male and female. This information, along with sexual preference and geographic location, is then filed into an algorithm that will present users with the mathematically most appropriate matches.


OkCupid, however, is breaking norms by allowing its users to select from over 20 genders, 13 orientations and pronoun selection options, allowing room for an array of possibilites rather than forcing any user into a box. The rest is essentially the same, and user information is still put through the algorithm to find effective matches, but much more inclusively.

A user-friendly experience?

Online dating, in its many capacities, has always acted as a safe-space for queer people of all identities to meet. However, when speaking with members of the community about their experiences in the world of online dating, there is a mixed-response about the inclusivity factor of dating pools found among services like OkCupid.


“I have found them to be discriminatory,” said Eric, 27, a genderqueer male-presenting person recently living in Philadelphia, PA. “I found that when I was using different dating apps that they would generally be the same individuals on all of them and that was challenging.” The discriminatory nature present in gay dating, in general, has been an issue over the past couple of years, as cited in several different reports, including this one by Vice “It didn’t feel like a productive use of my time.”


Shannon, a 30-year-old trans woman out of Pittsburgh, PA, expressed a similar sentiment: “I've lived in cities with pretty healthy queer scenes, but they're also connected to each other, so a lot of times people that I was meeting on OkCupid I had definitely been in a room with before and didn’t necessarily have a meaningful interaction with them then.”


Despite OkCupid’s inclusivity in its capability for users to select their own gender identity and sexual orientation, queer users have strayed from the app as of late for apps with more immediacy, like Grindr and Tinder.


"...having the first contact being rooted in some kind of mutual interest is more important to me, and that’s harder with OkCupid than with other apps."




Shannon’s experience is inherently different from Eric’s. There is a lot of danger that can come with being trans, and these dangers can multiply on the internet. Shannon voiced a preference for apps like Tinder and Grindr where the people that you interact with have already expressed an interest in you–a feature that hadn’t previously been employed by OkCupid until recently, through their updated swipe-capable phone app.


“I think for me I'm not... going to approach someone who hasn’t demonstrated any interest in me,” Shannon said. “Especially for trans women, a lot of the way our sexuality is talked about is in predatory terms, i.e. tricking men into sleeping with you, etc...having the first contact being rooted in some kind of mutual interest is more important to me, and that’s harder with OkCupid than with other apps.”


Eric conveyed a similar disinterest in the way that OkCupid operates differently than Tinder or Grindr. Until recently, OkCupid had functioned more like Facebook where users scroll through profiles, looking at multiple pictures and reading self-written bios. New apps brought a new-found sense of immediacy, which both Eric and Shannon preferred. “I just feel like with the introduction of Tinder and Grindr.. .the bandwidth of conversation became very short, where before I would have longer, more intimate conversations. It felt like things would escalate more quickly,” Eric said.


Though OkCupid is now more mobile-friendly, the fact that a lot of users still prefer to chat through the website can be a deterrent to people who are more interested in the instant gratification that the other services offer. “The people that I was having conversations with...it was very obvious that it felt like they were typing from a desktop and I was typing from my phone,” which Eric said made the whole experience more dissatisfying.


Eric and Shannon, regardless of their displeasure with the reach or inclusivity that OkCupid has in queer spaces, both spoke on the sense of community that they found in this space. They each cited that they made long-lasting friendships while using OkCupid and that that is perhaps the best thing that they gleaned from their time on the site. “I met a woman on OkCupid when I lived in Boston[, MA] that kind of, like, opened up a whole friend group to me of mostly trans women,” said Shannon. “Our relationship was and is platonic, and we realized that we had a lot in common…[that relationship] lead me to a community that I don’t know how I would have found otherwise.”

In Conclusion...

Gender and sexuality are evolving rapidly, and OkCupid is rebranding itself in an attempt to help a younger, queerer, more progressive audience find love through its tried-and-true algorithmic method. However, the old methods employed by the service function as a lingering deterrent for queer people who can be more easily protected by the immediacy, or even superficiality, of apps with more immediate results–like Tinder or Grindr. Regardless of its traditional uses, OkCupid has served as more of a community-building site for queer people across the map, which could render it even more useful than the intended purpose.


As society begins to grow into more progressive outlooks, so does the world of internet dating. Rufus Griscom for Wired Magazine predicted in 2002 that by 2022 people looking for love will inevitably look online, and the notion that they wouldn’t is “akin to skipping the card catalog to instead wander the stacks because ‘the right books are found only by accident.’” It looks like he’s right–it’s in the math.
